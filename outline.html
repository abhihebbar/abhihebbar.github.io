/mysite content outline

Clean editorial homepage structure.

This page is a content-only outline for the homepage. It focuses on structure, messaging, and what each section needs to say — not visual design.

1. Navigation

Keep navigation minimal and obvious.

  • Work
  • Notes
  • Contact

No extra cleverness needed here.

2. Hero

The hero should establish who Abhi is, what he does, and why it is worth reading further.

  • Short eyebrow line
  • Strong headline
  • One short intro paragraph
  • Optional second supporting paragraph
  • Two calm CTAs
Possible headline direction:
I build software, prototype ideas, and think through how products should work.

3. How I work

This section should explain the recurring themes in the work, without slipping into generic category labels.

  • Making ideas visible
  • Learning through experiments
  • Building better systems

The framing should feel like ways of working, not portfolio buckets.

4. Point of view

This section should make the homepage feel more specific and personal.

  • 3–5 short principles
  • Practical beliefs about prototyping, clarity, systems, and decision-making
  • One short closing line that reinforces the philosophy
Example principle direction:
Clarity usually comes from making something, not talking about it endlessly.

5. Selected notes

This section should give visitors a path deeper into Abhi’s thinking.

  • 3–4 selected links from the archive
  • One-line summaries for each
  • A clear link to browse the archive

This is proof of thought, not just a blog roll.

6. Contact

End with a calm, direct invitation to connect.

  • Short closing paragraph
  • Email
  • GitHub
  • LinkedIn
  • Optional Twitter if still relevant
Contact tone direction:
I like talking to people who are building interesting things.

7. Footer

Keep the footer simple and quiet.

  • Name
  • Optional short descriptor
  • Minimal secondary link if needed

Content rules

  • Keep it concise.
  • Sound specific, not broad or buzzwordy.
  • Feel personal, but not chatty.
  • Prefer clarity over clever phrasing.
  • Avoid startup-template language.
